        Spoilers without context: - jelly doughnuts - SIR YES SIR - soap - mickey mouse clubhouse! I will be completely honest. My ratings for Full Metal Jacket went up and down like a rollercoaster ride while viewing. It started as a 4 ; then going up to a 5 to only later drop to a 4.5; the 4.5 became a 3 but don't panic! My rating ended in a 4.5. I still cannot decipher why I cannot give it a full 5 but forget numbers. Full Metal Jacket is officially in the top 5 of Kubrick films. War films are nothing new but it's how you make something repetitive look new where you find cinema. He captures the dehumanization of a man in war. Every scene has an important message and we see a blend of cynical humor, (surprisingly) heart, and disaster all in one. Full Metal Jacket does a full damn circle. Raftman begins timid, not really getting in on the war action until the end when he kills the sniper. He yells loud and proud with curse words how happy he is for murdering the enemy. Joker expresses his desire to shoot a gun at an early age yet he's conflictive thoughout the film, whether it's not wanting to join in on the soap-beating of Private Pyle or leaving the sniper to die on her own. Animal is known as this rock with zero f**ks, a killing machine yet we almost see this as a facade of a man who is suffering on the inside from the war and whether or not he shows it, there's still some human in him. Pyle is an interesting character. We experience the slow destruction of a human finishing in the climatic bathroom scene. It's the ending for me. One can't help but chuckle to soldiers marching by burning buildings while singing a Mickey Mouse chant. But, the laughter is short because it is actually chilling. These men still are "kids," who went into a controversial and unnecessary war extremely soon in their lives and in the process lost a huge chunk of their being, yet there is still a tiny percentage of childhood left in them. Talk about an ending. Also, one can't help but hear Gunnery Sergeant's Hartman voice saying, "What is this Mickey Mouse horseshit?! "In short, I'm in a world of shit yes, but I am alive and I'm not afraid.

      • Spyro K
        Jun 28, 2021

        One of Kubrick's later films, I really consider this film his best attempt to portray the ramifications of war since his early Paths of Glory. The plot of the film follows American soldiers from their first time army train camps to their horrifying experiences in the Vietnam war. The film is distinctively divided in two parts, the first, where we are presented the difficul times of some soldiers named Gomer Pyle, Joker, Animal Mother etc. in an army camp, and their training by psychotic Gunnery Seargant Hartman (R.L. Ermey, in what I believe to be one of the greatest acting achievements in film and some of the best character acting on the screen, I cannot get enough of him) up until the murder of Hartman by Gomer Pyle, who lost his mind after incessant bullying by his peer and harsh training, and the latter's suicide. Up until the ending of the first part, the film maintained a rather dark comical tone, with some harrowing moments, but overall excellent improv by Ermey. The second part of the film follows the soldiers in an attempt to fight off Vietnamese troops, and despite losing friends, they manage to clear the area, but with serious casualties, such as their mental health (important to note that the camera always follows Private Joker and the repercussions war has had on him). The characteristic ending follows the soldiers in a burning landscape marching to their base while singing the Mickey Mouse Clubhouse song, quite ironic, innit. I have nothing but praise for everything, from the carefully adapted screenplay that takes a rather cliche story and elevates it to a whole new level, to technical achievements such as cinematography, art direction, editing and art direction, the sigature Kubrick direction and amazing work from the cast. Only complaint is that after the end of the first part, the tone of the film shifts so suddenly that it takes a while to get used to it, it just feels like the two parts are two different movies. But overall, great film, possibly one of the most darkly comical, harrowing, hard hitting, best examples of war cinema to come out in the 80s. YOU LITTLE SCUMBAG, I GOT YOUR NAME, I GOT YOUR @SS.
